Dual-KID Thermal-Inductive Feedback Concept

Hypothetical negative-feedback architecture using two series KIDs with opposite thermal-inductive response roles.

Concept Summary

Use two series inductive elements in one resonator, where KID1 is event-sensitive and KID2 is pre-biased hotter with heater control headroom.

An event raises \(T_1\), increasing \(L_1\), which increases detuning and reduces resonator readout power. Reduced readout power cools KID2, decreasing \(L_2\), which pulls total inductance back toward its pre-event value.

This forms a passive electrothermal detuning-compensation loop intended to keep the resonator close to the readout tone over a larger event range.

Event-to-Compensation Chain

\[ E_{\mathrm{event}}\uparrow \Rightarrow T_1\uparrow \Rightarrow L_1\uparrow \Rightarrow |x|\uparrow \Rightarrow P_0\downarrow \Rightarrow T_2\downarrow \Rightarrow L_2\downarrow \Rightarrow |x|\downarrow. \]

Desired effect: partial cancellation of total inductance shift,

\[ \Delta L_{\mathrm{tot}}=\Delta L_1+\Delta L_2,\qquad \Delta L_1>0,\ \Delta L_2<0. \]

Headroom Condition

KID2 must have enough available negative \(\Delta L_2\) swing to compensate KID1 event response.

Implemented same-temperature approximation rule in this project:

\[ \Delta T_{2,\mathrm{head}} \ge \frac{\alpha_{\phi1}R_1}{\alpha_{\phi2}R_2}\,\Delta T_{1,\mathrm{event}}, \]

with \(\Delta T_{2,\mathrm{head}}\) defined relative to KID2 zero-heater baseline temperature.

This is equivalent to requiring \(|\Delta L_{2,\mathrm{avail}}| \ge |\Delta L_{1,\mathrm{event}}|\) under the same-temperature extrapolation assumption for \(\alpha_\phi\).

Why It Could Help

  • Keeps resonator operating point near bias tone during events (larger linear dynamic range).
  • Reduces large detuning excursions that can otherwise compress response.
  • Potentially preserves readout gain over wider event amplitudes.

NEP Interpretation (Model Assumptions)

Under the current linear model assumptions, this compensation can improve dynamic-range behavior without necessarily degrading phase-channel NEP:

  • feedback appears only in the designated matrix term;
  • noise-source vectors are not directly feedback-dependent;
  • same source normalization is used for responsivity and NEP mapping.

See Feedback NEP Invariance for the formal cofactor-cancellation argument in the Q-channel case.

Caveats

  • This page describes a hypothesis-level architecture, not yet a complete nonlinear validation.
  • Small-signal matrix agreement does not guarantee large-signal lock robustness.
  • Practical heater/readout chain noise and control implementation details can change final NEP/performance.
